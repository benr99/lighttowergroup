CRE Finance Glossary

Plain-English definitions of the terms that matter in commercial real estate capital markets — from bridge loans to yield maintenance, DSCR to mezz debt.

No terms match your search. Try a different term.

B

Bridge Loan

Debt

A short-term, floating-rate loan secured by commercial real estate, typically used to finance transitional or value-add properties that don't yet qualify for permanent financing. Bridge loans usually carry 12–36 month terms with one or two extension options, float over SOFR, and provide leverage up to 75–80% LTV. They're structured to be repaid when the property is either sold or refinanced into permanent debt after stabilization. See also: Permanent Loan, Rate Cap, SOFR.

Related: Permanent Loan · Rate Cap · SOFR

Basis Points (bps)

General

A unit of measurement equal to 1/100th of 1%, commonly used in finance to describe interest rates, spreads, and fee changes. 100 basis points = 1%. Used to avoid ambiguity: saying "the rate increased 25 basis points" is more precise than "the rate increased 0.25%". Lenders quote spreads, loan fees (origination points), and yield maintenance calculations in basis points.

Bad Boy Carve-Outs

Loan Structure

Specific exceptions to non-recourse protection that make a borrower personally liable for loan losses if they commit certain acts. Standard carve-outs include: fraud, misappropriation of rents, environmental contamination, filing for bankruptcy without lender consent, material misrepresentation, and waste or destruction of the property. Most non-recourse CRE loans include bad boy carve-outs.

Related: Non-Recourse · Recourse

C

Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate)

Valuation

The ratio of a property's Net Operating Income (NOI) to its market value, expressed as a percentage. Formula: Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Value. A property generating $500,000 NOI with a market value of $10M has a 5.0% cap rate. Cap rates reflect risk and yield expectations — lower cap rates indicate higher valuations relative to income (growth assets or gateway markets); higher cap rates suggest more yield with greater risk or in secondary markets. Compressed cap rates mean values have risen relative to income.

Related: NOI · Value-Add

CMBS (Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities)

Debt

Fixed-rate commercial real estate loans that are pooled together and securitized into bonds sold to institutional investors. CMBS loans offer competitive fixed rates and are non-recourse, but come with rigid loan documents, yield maintenance or defeasance prepayment penalties, and limited post-closing flexibility. Best suited for stabilized, cash-flowing assets where the sponsor plans a long hold. Typical terms: 5, 7, or 10 years. Loan sizes typically $5M+.

Related: Yield Maintenance · Defeasance

Construction Loan

Debt

A short-term loan that finances the construction of new commercial real estate. Funds are disbursed in tranches (draws) as construction milestones are met and inspected. Typically floating rate, interest-only during the construction period, with a term matching the project's expected construction timeline (12–36 months). Upon completion and stabilization, the loan is repaid through a permanent refinancing or sale. Also called a "construction-to-perm" or "C&D" loan.

Related: Bridge Loan · Permanent Loan

Cash-on-Cash Return

Returns

Annual pre-tax cash flow distributed to equity investors divided by the total equity invested, expressed as a percentage. Measures the current income yield of an investment, distinct from total return which includes appreciation. A property generating $200K in annual cash flow on $2M of equity has a 10% cash-on-cash return. Interest-only loan structures increase cash-on-cash return relative to amortizing loans at the same rate.

D

DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)

Underwriting

The ratio of a property's Net Operating Income (NOI) to its annual debt service (principal + interest payments). Formula: DSCR = NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service. A property with $600,000 NOI and $500,000 annual debt service has a 1.20x DSCR. Most permanent lenders require a minimum DSCR of 1.20x–1.30x. A DSCR below 1.0x means the property doesn't generate enough income to cover its debt payments — a "cash flow negative" situation.

Related: NOI · LTV

Defeasance

Loan Structure

A prepayment mechanism, common in CMBS loans, that allows a borrower to prepay a fixed-rate loan by substituting government securities (Treasuries) for the mortgaged property. The borrower purchases a portfolio of Treasury securities that match the remaining loan cash flows. The cost of defeasance is effectively the present value of the rate differential between the loan rate and current Treasury yields. Can be extremely expensive if market rates have fallen since origination.

Related: CMBS · Yield Maintenance

Debt Yield

Underwriting

A lender underwriting metric equal to a property's NOI divided by the total loan amount, expressed as a percentage. Formula: Debt Yield = NOI ÷ Loan Amount. Unlike DSCR, debt yield is not affected by interest rates or amortization schedules — it measures the lender's return on the principal invested if they took ownership of the property. Permanent lenders typically require minimum debt yields of 7–9% depending on asset type. Increasingly used as the primary sizing constraint for CMBS.

E

Equity Multiple

Returns

Total equity distributions received (including return of capital) divided by total equity invested. A 2.0x equity multiple means investors received twice their initial investment back over the hold period, including both income distributions and sale proceeds. Unlike IRR, equity multiple doesn't account for the time value of money — a 2.0x multiple in 3 years is far more attractive than a 2.0x multiple in 10 years.

Related: IRR · Cash-on-Cash Return

F

Floating Rate vs. Fixed Rate

Loan Structure

A floating rate loan (also called variable rate) has an interest rate that adjusts periodically based on a benchmark index (typically SOFR) plus a fixed spread. Bridge loans are almost always floating rate. A fixed rate loan maintains the same interest rate for the life of the loan, providing payment predictability. CMBS, agency (Fannie/Freddie), and life company loans are typically fixed rate. Fixed rate loans carry prepayment penalties (yield maintenance or defeasance) that protect lenders from reinvestment risk.

G

Guaranty (Personal Guaranty)

Loan Structure

A legal commitment by an individual (the guarantor, typically a sponsor or principal) to personally repay a loan if the borrowing entity defaults. A full recourse guaranty means the guarantor is personally liable for the entire loan balance. A limited guaranty caps personal exposure at a dollar amount or percentage, or limits it to specific events (bad boy carve-outs). Non-recourse loans have no personal guaranty beyond carve-outs.

Related: Non-Recourse · Bad Boy Carve-Outs

I

IO (Interest-Only)

Loan Structure

A loan structure where monthly payments consist only of interest, with no principal reduction (amortization) during the IO period. The full original principal balance is due at maturity. IO periods can be partial-term (e.g., 5 years IO then 25-year amortization on a 30-year loan) or full-term (IO for the entire loan duration). Full-term IO significantly reduces annual debt service, increasing DSCR and cash-on-cash return — a primary driver of underwriting and return modeling.

Related: DSCR · Cash-on-Cash Return

IRR (Internal Rate of Return)

Returns

The annualized rate of return that makes the net present value (NPV) of all cash flows from an investment equal to zero. The IRR accounts for both the magnitude and timing of cash flows, making it the primary return metric in real estate private equity and institutional underwriting. Target IRRs for CRE investments typically range from 12–20%+ depending on risk profile. Heavily influenced by leverage, hold period, and exit cap rate assumptions.

L

LTV (Loan-to-Value)

Underwriting

The ratio of the loan amount to the appraised value (or purchase price) of the property, expressed as a percentage. Formula: LTV = Loan Amount ÷ Property Value. A $7M loan on a $10M property = 70% LTV. Maximum LTV limits vary by lender and loan type: agency permanent loans typically max at 75–80%; bridge loans at 75–80%; CMBS at 65–75%; life companies at 55–65%. Higher LTV = more leverage for the borrower = more risk for the lender.

Related: DSCR · Debt Yield

LTC (Loan-to-Cost)

Underwriting

Used in construction and value-add lending, LTC is the ratio of the loan amount to the total project cost (purchase price + renovation or construction budget + soft costs). Formula: LTC = Loan Amount ÷ Total Project Cost. Lenders use LTC to measure their exposure relative to the sponsor's all-in investment. Construction lenders typically max at 65–75% LTC, ensuring the sponsor has meaningful equity at risk.

Life Company (Life Insurance Company)

Debt Sources

Life insurance companies are among the most conservative and sought-after sources of permanent CRE financing. They invest insurance reserve funds in long-term, fixed-rate mortgages with conservative LTV (typically 55–65%) and strong DSCR requirements. Life company loans typically offer the lowest rates in the permanent market, non-recourse structure, flexible prepayment options, and fewer reserves compared to CMBS. Best for high-quality, stabilized assets with low leverage needs.

M

Mezzanine Debt (Mezz)

Debt

A layer of financing that sits between senior debt and equity in the capital stack. Mezzanine debt is typically structured as a loan secured not by a mortgage on the property, but by a pledge of the ownership interests (LLC membership interests) in the property-owning entity. Mezz lenders have the right to "cure" a senior loan default and foreclose on the ownership interests. Mezz carries higher interest rates than senior debt (often 10–14%+) reflecting its subordinate position and higher risk.

Related: Preferred Equity · LTV

Maturity / Extension

Loan Structure

The scheduled date on which a loan's full principal balance becomes due. Bridge loans often include extension options (typically one to two 12-month extensions) that the borrower can exercise if certain conditions are met (minimum DSCR, minimum occupancy, purchase of a rate cap, payment of extension fees). Failure to repay or refinance at maturity results in a default.

N

NOI (Net Operating Income)

Underwriting

A property's total revenue minus operating expenses, before debt service, capital expenditures, depreciation, and income taxes. Formula: NOI = Gross Revenue – Operating Expenses. NOI is the foundational metric in CRE underwriting — cap rates, DSCR, debt yield, and valuations all derive from NOI. Understanding what lenders include and exclude in their NOI calculation (management fees, reserves, vacancy assumptions) is critical to structuring a successful financing.

Related: Cap Rate · DSCR · Debt Yield

Non-Recourse

Loan Structure

A loan structure in which the lender's only remedy upon default is to foreclose on the property — they cannot pursue the borrower's other assets or personal balance sheet (except for bad boy carve-outs). Non-recourse is standard in CMBS, agency, and life company loans. Bridge loans may be non-recourse or full/limited recourse depending on the lender and deal. Non-recourse provides significant downside protection for sponsors with diversified portfolios.

Related: Bad Boy Carve-Outs · Guaranty

P

Permanent Loan

Debt

A long-term, fixed-rate mortgage on a stabilized commercial property, typically with a 5–30 year term. Common sources include agency lenders (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac for multifamily), CMBS, life insurance companies, and commercial banks. Permanent loans require stabilized occupancy, proven cash flow, and conservative leverage (typically 55–75% LTV). They carry meaningful prepayment penalties to protect lenders from reinvestment risk. The "take-out" loan that replaces a bridge loan upon stabilization.

Related: Bridge Loan · CMBS

Preferred Equity

Equity

A hybrid capital instrument that sits between senior debt and common equity in the capital stack, structured as an equity interest rather than a mortgage loan. Preferred equity holders receive a fixed preferred return (often 8–14%) and have rights to receive distributions before common equity holders receive anything. Unlike mezzanine debt, preferred equity is structured as an equity investment — preferred holders don't foreclose through a mortgage but often have rights to remove the sponsor upon certain defaults.

Related: Mezzanine Debt

Prepayment Penalty / Step-Down

Loan Structure

A fee charged when a borrower repays a loan before its scheduled maturity. Common structures include: Yield Maintenance (make-whole to the lender's yield, most expensive); Defeasance (substitute government securities, common in CMBS); Step-Down (declining percentage: e.g., 5-4-3-2-1%, decreasing each year); and Spread Maintenance (calculated to maintain the lender's spread over a benchmark). Step-down structures are most flexible for sponsors with uncertain hold periods.

Related: Yield Maintenance · Defeasance

R

Recourse

Loan Structure

A loan in which the lender can pursue the borrower's personal assets beyond the mortgaged property in the event of a default. Full recourse means the borrower is personally liable for the entire loan balance if the property proceeds are insufficient. Common in smaller bank construction and bridge loans, especially for sponsors without established track records. The rate premium for non-recourse financing is almost always worth it for sponsors with diversified personal balance sheets.

Rate Cap

Risk Management

An interest rate derivative that limits the maximum interest rate a borrower pays on a floating rate loan. The borrower pays an upfront premium to a counterparty who agrees to compensate them if the benchmark rate exceeds the cap strike rate. Bridge lenders typically require rate caps as a loan condition. Cost and availability of caps are driven by current market rates, volatility, term, and cap strike level. In a rising rate environment, rate cap costs increase substantially.

S

SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate)

Market Rate

The primary floating rate benchmark used in U.S. commercial lending since replacing LIBOR in 2023. SOFR is based on overnight repurchase agreements backed by U.S. Treasury securities and is published by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. Bridge loans price as "SOFR + spread" — for example, a loan at "SOFR + 300 bps" with SOFR at 5.30% means an all-in rate of 8.30%. SOFR varies daily; term SOFR rates (1-month, 3-month) are used for loan payment calculations.

Spread

Pricing

The incremental interest rate charged by a lender above a benchmark index, representing the lender's margin for risk, operating costs, and profit. For fixed-rate loans, spread is quoted over a comparable-maturity Treasury yield (e.g., "T+175bps"). For floating rate loans, spread is over SOFR. Spread compression means lenders are accepting thinner margins — indicating high demand, competition, or improving credit conditions. Spread widening signals tightening credit markets or perceived risk increase.

T

Term Sheet / LOI

Process

A non-binding document issued by a lender or equity provider that outlines the proposed terms of a financing: loan amount, rate/spread, term, LTV, recourse, prepayment, reserves, fees, and major conditions. Term sheets are issued after initial review of the deal and indicate the lender's serious interest. They serve as the basis for negotiation before a commitment letter (binding) is issued. Key items to scrutinize: rate index and floor, fee structure, prepayment, and all reserve requirements.

V

Value-Add

Strategy

A real estate investment strategy that involves acquiring a property with below-market performance and improving it through renovation, lease-up, operational improvements, or repositioning to increase NOI and value. Value-add properties typically require bridge financing during the improvement phase, then refinance into permanent debt or sell at a higher cap-rate-implied value after stabilization. Risk is higher than core or core-plus strategies but returns should be commensurately higher.

Y

Yield Maintenance

Loan Structure

A prepayment penalty structure that compensates the lender for the interest income lost when a borrower repays a fixed-rate loan early. The calculation produces the present value of the remaining loan payments at the loan's contract rate, discounted at the current Treasury yield — effectively "making the lender whole" on their expected return. Yield maintenance can be extremely costly when prevailing interest rates have fallen significantly below the loan's contract rate, making early payoff very expensive and cash-out refinancings unattractive.

Related: Defeasance · Prepayment Penalty · CMBS
