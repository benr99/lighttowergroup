CRE Deal Submission Checklist

The 15 items every lender needs before they can seriously underwrite your commercial real estate deal. Get these together before your first lender call and you'll move four times faster.

Why This Matters

Most deals slow down not because of bad fundamentals, but because of missing information. A lender who can't underwrite can't issue a term sheet. A lender who sends a lengthy due diligence list two weeks in is a lender signaling that your deal isn't a priority yet.

Sponsors who come to market with a complete package get faster responses, better terms, and more competitive execution — because the lender can size the deal in their first review rather than issuing a request for more information. The 15 items below are what institutional lenders expect before they issue an LOI.

The 15-Item Checklist

  1. 01

    Executive Summary Required

    1–2 pages covering: asset type and location, sponsor background, business plan overview, loan request (amount, structure, term), and the investment thesis. Write this for someone who has never heard of your deal — they need to understand it in 90 seconds.

  2. 02

    Rent Roll Required

    Current unit-level rent roll with: unit mix and square footage, lease start and expiration dates, in-place rents, and market rents. For commercial properties: tenant name, suite, lease term, base rent, and escalation schedule.

  3. 03

    Trailing 12-Month (T-12) Financials Required

    12 months of actual operating income and expense statements. This is the lender's starting point. Pro-forma projections alone won't get you a serious LOI — lenders underwrite to what the property is actually producing today.

  4. 04

    3-Year Historical Financials Required

    Annual income and expense statements for the prior three years (or from acquisition if held less than three years). This shows income and expense trends, unusual one-time items, and whether the T-12 is representative or an outlier.

  5. 05

    Pro-Forma Operating Statement Required

    Stabilized income and expense projections with clearly stated assumptions. Lenders will pick this apart — make sure your vacancy assumption, management fee, reserves, and expense ratios are defensible against market data.

  6. 06

    Sources and Uses Required

    Total project cost (purchase price + renovation + closing costs + carry) versus total funding (senior debt + equity + other). Every dollar must be accounted for on both sides. Lenders look for equity comfort — the gap between their loan and your all-in basis.

  7. 07

    Purchase Contract or Basis Documentation Required

    For acquisitions: the fully executed purchase and sale agreement (PSA) with all amendments and addenda. For refinances: evidence of original purchase price and a clear accounting of capital invested since acquisition.

  8. 08

    Property Description and Photos Required

    Address, year built, total square footage (or unit count), parking, major amenities, recent capital improvements completed, and current condition. Include interior and exterior photos — lenders make snap judgments based on visuals. Bad photos kill deals.

  9. 09

    Market Rent Comparables Required

    Rent comps supporting your in-place and projected rents. For value-add deals, you need comps showing where renovated units are leasing — not just the asset class average. A grid of 5–8 comps with property name, unit type, rent, and distance is the standard format.

  10. 10

    Sponsor Résumé / Track Record Required

    A deal-by-deal list of prior transactions: property type, market, total cost, loan amount, current status, and outcome if sold or refinanced. Lenders lend to sponsors as much as to properties. A clean, honest track record is one of your strongest underwriting assets.

  11. 11

    Sponsor Liquidity Statement Required

    A current personal financial statement (PFS) or fund-level balance sheet showing net worth and liquid assets. Most lenders require post-closing liquidity of 10–15% of the loan amount. Have your most recent brokerage and bank statements ready to back this up.

  12. 12

    Business Plan (Value-Add Deals) Preferred

    For bridge loan requests: a clear renovation plan with unit-level scope, cost per unit, projected rent lift, phasing timeline, and projected stabilization date. Lenders are underwriting your exit, not your current NOI — they need to believe in the business plan to size the loan confidently.

  13. 13

    Capital Improvement Budget Preferred

    Itemized renovation or construction budget with contractor bids or detailed estimates. Line out hard costs (construction), soft costs (architecture, permits, FF&E), and contingency reserve. Lenders will hold back renovation proceeds in draws — they need this to build the holdback schedule.

  14. 14

    Appraisal or Broker Opinion of Value Preferred

    A current third-party MAI appraisal is ideal but often not yet completed at LOI stage. A broker opinion of value (BOV) from a reputable local firm is acceptable at the initial stage. Lenders will order their own appraisal in due diligence, but third-party support of value strengthens your initial package.

  15. 15

    Exit Strategy Required

    How and when you plan to exit the loan: refinance into permanent financing upon stabilization, sell at a target cap rate, or long-term hold with agency takeout. Include the assumptions behind your exit (stabilized NOI, exit cap rate, proceeds, and coverage on permanent debt). The cleaner your exit underwriting, the more confident a lender is in your request.

A Few Practical Notes

Submit what you have. You don't need all 15 items to start a conversation. Items 1–11 are typically sufficient for a lender to issue a preliminary term sheet. Items 12–15 support deeper diligence for complex transactions. Don't delay outreach waiting for the perfect package — a good broker can work with what you have and help you identify what's missing.

Organize into a deal folder, not a zip file. Label your documents clearly: "01 Executive Summary," "02 Rent Roll," etc. A well-organized folder signals a professional sponsor. An unorganized zip file with files named "final_v3_REVISED.xls" signals the opposite.

Anticipate follow-up questions. Every lender will ask about occupancy trends, major tenants, deferred maintenance, and why you're in the deal at your basis. Think through your answers before your first call — confident, data-backed answers to these questions move deals forward.
