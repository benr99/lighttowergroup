How Lenders Calculate Maximum Loan Size: NOI, Debt Yield, and DSCR Working Together

Three separate constraints determine how much a lender will put on a commercial property. The loan amount you actually get is always the lowest of the three — even if two of them would support a much larger number. Here's how to understand and engineer all three.

By Benjamin Rohr February 2026 10 min read

Most sponsors understand that lenders have a maximum LTV — and assume that if their deal stays below it, they can get the loan. This is correct but incomplete. Lenders size loans using three interlocking constraints, and the actual loan amount is determined by whichever one is most restrictive.

The three constraints are:

  1. Loan-to-Value (LTV) — the loan as a percentage of appraised value
  2. Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) — how much income coverage remains after debt payments
  3. Debt Yield — the property's NOI as a percentage of the loan amount

Each one constrains the loan from a different angle. LTV controls risk relative to collateral value. DSCR controls cash flow adequacy. Debt yield controls yield-on-cost to the lender — and is increasingly the dominant constraint in today's high-rate environment.

The Rule: Your maximum loan amount is always the lowest number produced when you apply all three constraints independently. No amount of strength in two of them can overcome a fail in the third.

Constraint 1: Loan-to-Value (LTV)

Loan-to-value is the most intuitive constraint. The lender will lend up to a certain percentage of the appraised value — and not more, regardless of cash flow. This protects the lender in a foreclosure scenario: if they need to recover the loan from a distressed sale, the collateral should be worth enough to cover it.

Formula
LTV = Loan Amount ÷ Appraised Value
A $6.5M loan on a $10M building = 65% LTV

Maximum LTV varies significantly by lender type, loan type, and market conditions. In 2026, typical maximum LTVs run from 55–65% for conservative permanent lenders (life companies) to 75–80% for bridge lenders on transitional assets. Agency lenders on multifamily typically cap at 75–80% LTV depending on loan program.

The critical subtlety: LTV only matters as much as the appraisal is accurate. In falling markets, appraised values lag actual market values — which is why lenders added the other two constraints as backstops. A property can be at 65% LTV but still have insufficient cash flow to support the debt service, which is what DSCR protects against.

Constraint 2: Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

DSCR measures whether the property generates enough income to service its debt — with a cushion. A DSCR of 1.25x means the property generates $1.25 in net operating income for every $1.00 of annual debt service. The cushion protects against vacancy spikes, expense increases, or rent softness.

Formula
DSCR = NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service
$650,000 NOI ÷ $490,000 Debt Service = 1.33x DSCR

Because DSCR constrains the loan via the debt service payment — which is a function of both loan amount and interest rate — it interacts with interest rates in powerful ways. When rates rise, the debt service on any given loan amount increases, which means DSCR falls, which means the maximum loan amount that satisfies the minimum DSCR also falls.

This is the mechanism behind the denominator problem that reshaped the lending market in 2022–2024: the same property with the same NOI suddenly required a materially smaller loan to stay within lender DSCR minimums, simply because rates had moved 300–400 basis points.

To find the maximum loan from DSCR alone, you work backwards:

  1. Take your property's stabilized NOI
  2. Divide by the minimum DSCR (e.g. 1.25)
  3. That gives you the maximum allowable annual debt service
  4. Back into the loan amount using your interest rate and amortization

For example: $650,000 NOI ÷ 1.25 minimum DSCR = $520,000 maximum allowable annual debt service. At a 6.5% I/O rate, that supports a maximum loan of $8,000,000.

Constraint 3: Debt Yield

Debt yield is the lender's measure of raw return on their capital — independent of appraised value and independent of interest rates. It compares what the property earns to what the lender has deployed.

Formula
Debt Yield = NOI ÷ Loan Amount
$650,000 NOI ÷ $7,500,000 Loan = 8.67% Debt Yield

Why does this matter as a constraint? Because it's rate-independent. Unlike DSCR, debt yield doesn't move when rates change. It tells the lender: if I had to foreclose on this asset tomorrow and operate it, what return would I earn on my basis? Minimum debt yield floors (typically 7–10% depending on lender type and asset class) ensure the lender's yield-on-cost is always adequate regardless of what happens in the rate environment.

To find the maximum loan from debt yield alone: divide your stabilized NOI by the minimum debt yield requirement. $650,000 NOI ÷ 9% minimum debt yield = $7,222,222 maximum loan.

To increase the maximum loan that debt yield allows, you need to increase NOI — which is exactly why value-add business plans often improve borrowing capacity even beyond what appraised value alone would support.

How They Interact: A Worked Example

Let's put all three constraints on a single property and see which one bites:

Property Detail Value
Stabilized NOI $650,000
Appraised Value $10,000,000
Interest Rate 6.5% (I/O)
Annual Debt Service @ $7M loan $455,000

Now apply each constraint:

Constraint Lender Minimum Calculation Max Loan
LTV 65% 65% × $10,000,000 $6,500,000
DSCR 1.25x $650K ÷ 1.25 = $520K max DS → ÷ 6.5% $8,000,000
Debt Yield 9.0% $650,000 ÷ 9% $7,222,000
The Binding Constraint

In this deal, LTV is the binding constraint at $6.5M — even though DSCR would allow $8M and debt yield would allow $7.2M. The sponsor's maximum loan is $6,500,000, regardless of what the other two metrics say.

This happens frequently when appraised values don't keep pace with NOI growth. The property may be generating excellent cash flow, but if the appraisal caps it, you're capped regardless.

Now let's shift interest rates up 100 basis points to 7.5%:

Constraint At 6.5% At 7.5% Change
LTV Max $6,500,000 $6,500,000 No change
DSCR Max $8,000,000 $6,933,000 −$1,067,000
Debt Yield Max $7,222,000 $7,222,000 No change

Rate sensitivity falls entirely on DSCR. Both LTV and debt yield are unaffected by rate changes — which is precisely why lenders use debt yield as a rate-independent underwriting floor. At 7.5%, DSCR now becomes the binding constraint on deals where it was previously permissive, compressing loan proceeds.

Minimum Thresholds by Lender Type

Different lenders use different minimums — and understanding who prices where is the job of your capital advisor.

Lender Type Max LTV Min DSCR Min Debt Yield Notes
Agency (Fannie/Freddie) 75–80% 1.20–1.25x 6.5–7.5% Multifamily only; best rates
CMBS 65–75% 1.20–1.25x 8.0–9.0% Non-recourse; prepayment restrictions
Life Insurance Company 55–65% 1.40–1.50x 8.0–9.5% Conservative; lowest rates for quality assets
Regional Bank 65–75% 1.20–1.35x 8.0–9.0% Recourse; relationship-driven; flexible
Bridge/Debt Fund 70–80% 1.05–1.15x 7.0–8.5% Higher rate; short-term; transitional assets

The gap between agency and life company on LTV is notable — an 80% LTV agency loan can generate substantially more proceeds than a 60% LTV life company loan on the same asset. But agency is limited to multifamily, and life company rates are often a full 50–100 basis points lower, so the after-debt-service cash position may be better at lower leverage. This is the capital structure tradeoff analysis your advisor should be running for every deal.

Engineering Around the Constraints

Understanding the three constraints gives sponsors a framework for improving loan proceeds — not by gaming the underwriting, but by genuinely improving the deal's profile:

Increase NOI

NOI is the single lever that improves all three constraints simultaneously. Higher NOI lifts DSCR (more coverage at the same debt service), improves debt yield (more income relative to loan), and often increases appraised value (and therefore the LTV ceiling). Value-add strategies that lift rents through renovation are capital formation strategies — they directly expand borrowing capacity.

Right-Size the Loan Request

Some sponsors go to market asking for more leverage than the deal supports. A well-structured ask — one that comes in cleanly under all three constraints with room to spare — gets better execution. Lenders don't fight over deals that stress their underwriting. They compete aggressively for deals that underwrite cleanly.

Choose the Right Lender Type

Because different lenders have different constraint floors, the right lender for your deal is not always obvious. A life company that requires 9.5% debt yield may pass on a deal that a CMBS shop would happily fund at 8.5% debt yield — at a lower rate. Mapping your deal against the full lender universe reveals who should be competing for it. This is the work your CRE capital advisor should be doing upfront.

Layer the Capital Stack

When senior loan proceeds are insufficient to make a deal work — because one or more constraints is binding — the solution is often mezzanine debt or preferred equity to fill the gap. A senior loan at 65% LTV plus mezzanine to 80% LTV brings total proceeds to the same place a bridge lender might go, but with different cost and structure characteristics. The right answer depends on the business plan and exit timing.

What This Means for Sponsors

The practical takeaway: every loan sizing conversation should start with three calculations, not one. Know your LTV ceiling, know your DSCR-implied maximum, know your debt yield-implied maximum. The binding constraint is your real answer — and it tells you exactly what you need to improve if you want more capital.

The deeper insight: capital advisory is not just lender sourcing. It's constraint identification, structure engineering, and lender matching — run simultaneously. The right advisor understands not only who lends, but exactly what each lender needs to see, and how to position your specific deal so that the right lenders compete aggressively for it.
