The Rate Shopping Trap

Rate shopping treats capital as a commodity. It assumes that a loan is a loan is a loan, and the only differentiator is price. This logic works when you're comparing identical products â€” two gallons of milk at different stores. But commercial real estate loans are not identical products.

A 6.25% loan from Lender A and a 6.35% loan from Lender B may look like a clear choice â€” until you read the term sheets. Lender A requires a 12-month yield maintenance, full recourse, and a 60-day close. Lender B offers a declining prepayment schedule, limited recourse, and can close in 30 days. Which is the better loan?

The answer depends on your business plan. And that's the point.

Structure Determines Outcomes

The elements of loan structure that matter more than rate include:

Prepayment Flexibility

If you plan to sell or refinance in 3â€“5 years, the prepayment structure of your loan will have a larger impact on your exit economics than the rate. A loan with yield maintenance can cost millions to prepay. A loan with a declining prepayment penalty â€” or a window for free prepayment â€” preserves your optionality.

Interest-Only Period

A full-term interest-only loan on a $30 million mortgage saves approximately $500,000â€“$700,000 in annual debt service compared to a 30-year amortizing loan at the same rate. That cash flow goes directly to distributions, reserves, or capital improvements. An extra 10 basis points in rate is de minimis compared to the value of IO.

Recourse vs. Non-Recourse

The economic value of non-recourse financing is difficult to quantify â€” until something goes wrong. In a downturn, non-recourse carve-outs limit the sponsor's exposure to specific "bad boy" acts. Full recourse exposes the sponsor's personal balance sheet to the full loss. The rate premium for non-recourse is almost always worth it.

Reserves and Holdbacks

How the lender structures operating reserves, tax escrows, insurance escrows, and renovation holdbacks affects your cash-on-cash return and your ability to execute the business plan. A lender who structures reserves thoughtfully â€” releasing them as conditions are met â€” creates more value than one who locks up cash in inflexible escrows.

Certainty of Close

Perhaps the most undervalued element of capital advisory is certainty of close. In competitive markets, the ability to close on time with the capital you committed is worth far more than a lower rate from a lender who may retrade, delay, or fail to perform.

I've seen sponsors lose deals â€” deals they won in "best and final" â€” because their lender couldn't close on time. I've seen sponsors accept retraded terms at closing because they had no alternative. These failures are rarely about rate. They're about choosing a capital partner based on rate rather than execution quality.

The Right Question

The question isn't "what's the lowest rate I can get?" The question is "what is the optimal capital structure for my business plan, and who can I trust to execute it?"
