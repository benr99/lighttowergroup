Debt Yield vs. LTV: The Metric Lenders Now Trust More

By Benjamin Rohr · February 2026 ·8 min read

Ask a seasoned debt fund underwriter what they care about most and they almost certainly won't say LTV. They'll say debt yield. It's been standard in institutional lending for years, but the 2022–2024 rate cycle accelerated its adoption as the primary underwriting benchmark — particularly for bridge and permanent debt. Here's what it is, how to calculate it, and why it changes how you should think about sizing your deal.

The Shift Away from LTV

For most of the past two decades, loan-to-value was the dominant sizing constraint in CRE lending. A lender would appraise a property, calculate a value, and lend up to a defined percentage. Simple, intuitive, and deeply embedded in the industry's vocabulary.

The problem is that appraised value is itself a function of interest rates. When cap rates compress, property values rise — which makes LTV appear more conservative than it actually is. A lender who made a 70% LTV loan against a property valued at $15M in 2019 was making a very different risk calculation when the same property was valued at $22M in 2021 due to cap rate compression from 6% to 4%.

When rates spiked in 2022 and values corrected, those loans turned out to be deeply underwater. The LTV buffer evaporated. Debt yield survived. That experience accelerated a shift already underway at the institutional level: away from appraisal-dependent LTV, toward a metric that doesn't lie.

What Is Debt Yield?

Debt yield measures the relationship between a property's income and the total loan amount — independent of interest rates, cap rates, or appraised value. It tells a lender what return they would earn on the loan if they took the property back today and operated it as-is.

The Formula

Debt Yield = NOI ÷ Loan Amount

No appraised value. No cap rate. No interest rate assumption. Just current net operating income divided by the loan amount requested — expressed as a percentage.

A Worked Example

A stabilized multifamily property with an NOI of $1,200,000. Sponsor requests a $15,000,000 loan.

Metric Value Note
NOI $1,200,000 In-place, stabilized
Loan Amount $15,000,000 Requested proceeds
Debt Yield 8.0% $1.2M ÷ $15M
Appraised Value (@ 5.5% cap) $21,818,000 Not used in debt yield

The appraised value — and the LTV — don't enter the calculation. A lender relying on debt yield is insulated from the distorting effect of cap rate compression on property values.

Why Lenders Prefer It

The Core LTV Problem

LTV is appraisal-dependent, and appraisals use cap rates drawn from comparables — reflecting current market conditions. When cap rates fall, LTV gets permissive. When they rise, it gets restrictive. This procyclical behavior has burned lenders: loans originated at peak-inflated values default during corrections.

Debt yield is acyclical. A 9% debt yield means the same thing whether cap rates are 4% or 7%.

Rate-Independent Underwriting

The 2022–2023 rate spike also exposed a problem with DSCR-based underwriting: when a floating-rate loan moves from 4% to 9%, debt service changes dramatically and DSCR collapses. Debt yield is entirely rate-independent — it doesn't care what the interest rate is. Lenders burned by DSCR deterioration have increasingly weighted debt yield as their primary sizing constraint.

Debt Yield Minimums by Lender Type

Lender Type Min Debt Yield Asset Class
Agency (Fannie/Freddie) 6.5–7.5% Multifamily
CMBS / Conduit 7.5–9.0% All commercial
Life Company 7.0–8.5% Core / Core-Plus
Debt Fund (Bridge) 7.0–9.0% All transitional
Regional Bank 8.0–10.0% All, with relationship

These are guidelines, not hard rules. Sponsor quality, market, asset type, and loan structure can move thresholds in either direction.

Debt Yield vs. DSCR: When Each Governs

Sophisticated lenders use both. DSCR measures income relative to debt service — a cash flow coverage metric sensitive to interest rates. Debt yield measures income relative to loan balance — a balance sheet metric that is rate-agnostic.

In a low-rate environment, DSCR is typically the binding constraint. In a high-rate environment, debt yield becomes binding. In today's environment — rates elevated but declining — both often bind simultaneously. Sponsors need to know which one limits their proceeds before approaching lenders.

What This Means for Sponsors

  • Model the binding constraint, not the aspirational LTV. Before assuming 75% LTV, calculate what loan amount achieves the lender's minimum debt yield on your current NOI. That's your true maximum loan.
  • NOI quality matters more than ever. Lenders underwrite debt yield on in-place, stabilized NOI — not pro-forma. On value-add deals, the loan is sized to current NOI with additional proceeds released as income improves.
  • Understand vacancy assumptions. Lenders apply a standard vacancy haircut to gross rents before computing NOI. A 10% vs. 5% vacancy assumption meaningfully changes debt yield and, therefore, proceeds.
  • Budget for the gap. If debt yield caps your loan below the LTV number, you need more equity. Model both constraints and know which one governs before you go to market.

Bottom Line

Debt yield isn't replacing every other underwriting metric — DSCR still governs in low-rate environments and LTV still matters for regulatory purposes. But any sponsor who reaches the capital markets in 2026 without calculating debt yield first is flying blind. The lender is almost certainly starting there. You should too.

The best outcome is when debt yield, DSCR, and LTV all pass simultaneously — that signals genuine credit quality across all three lenses. When one binds the others, the structure needs to change. Knowing this before you reach out allows a productive first conversation rather than a painful retrade at the term sheet stage.
