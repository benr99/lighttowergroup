The Bridge Lending Landscape in 2026: Who's Lending, at What Terms, and Why It Matters

By Benjamin Rohr · February 2026 · 9 min read

The bridge lending market of 2026 looks nothing like it did in 2021. Cheap floating-rate debt at 300–350 basis points over SOFR and 80% LTV is gone. What replaced it is more disciplined, more selective — and for sponsors who understand the landscape, more navigable than the chaos of 2023 and 2024. Here is what is actually happening in the bridge market right now, and what it means for sponsors with transitional assets.

Who Is Lending in 2026

The bridge lending market is not monolithic. Understanding which lenders are most active — and what each category values — is the starting point for a successful financing strategy.

Debt Funds: Still the Primary Bridge Capital Source

Debt funds — non-bank lenders typically backed by institutional investors, credit funds, or insurance capital — remain the dominant force in bridge lending. Unlike banks, they aren't constrained by regulatory capital requirements or CRE concentration limits, which means they can move faster, structure more flexibly, and lend on assets that fall outside bank credit boxes.

The proliferation of debt funds between 2018 and 2022 created a competitive, sophisticated market. Today's leading debt funds include household names like Blackstone Real Estate Debt Strategies, KKR Real Estate Finance Trust, Mesa West, Benefit Street Partners, and dozens of mid-market platforms with $500M–$5B of capital. The field has consolidated somewhat from its 2021–2022 peak, with weaker platforms exiting as their portfolios stressed during the rate spike. What remains is a stronger cohort of lenders with deeper underwriting bench and more institutional capital sources.

What debt funds want in 2026: Experienced sponsors. Proven business plans — not speculative upside. Markets they know. Clear exit strategies (either a sale at a defined basis or a refinancing path with conservative assumptions). They are underwriting to stress scenarios, not best-case projections.

Regional Banks: A Meaningful Return

One of the most significant developments in the 2026 lending environment is the re-entry of regional banks into transitional lending — cautiously, but meaningfully. After dramatically pulling back in 2023 following the Silicon Valley Bank collapse and subsequent concerns about CRE concentrations, many regional banks have rebuilt capital ratios, resolved problem assets, and begun actively seeking new bridge production.

Regional bank bridge loans have a different profile than debt fund loans. They typically feature lower proceeds (65–70% LTV versus 75–80% for debt funds), more conservative underwriting, relationship requirements, and personal guaranty in many cases. But they also offer something debt funds often can't: relationship banking, flexible loan structures, and rates that can be meaningfully lower for strong sponsors with existing bank relationships.

For sponsors in the $5M–$30M deal range with established banking relationships, the re-entry of regional banks has materially improved competition and pricing.

Life Companies: Selective Bridge Activity

Some life insurance companies have entered the bridge market for transitional assets that are close to permanent financing quality — well-located, strong sponsor, minimal remaining business plan execution risk. These aren't your typical value-add bridge loans; they're closer to "light transitional" deals where the life company is comfortable holding the risk. Rates are competitive and execution is highly professional, but the credit boxes are narrow.

How Bridge Terms Have Changed Since 2022

The bridge market of 2021–2022 was a lender's market in disguise — so many lenders were competing so aggressively that terms compressed to sponsor-friendly extremes. That era is over. Here's a category-by-category breakdown of where terms stand today.

Spreads

In 2021, well-sponsored multifamily bridge deals were pricing at SOFR + 250–325 bps. Today, market clearing spreads for comparable deals are SOFR + 325–450 bps, with the best-in-class sponsors on well-located stabilizing assets at the lower end and transitional/value-add assets in tertiary markets at the higher end.

The compression from the 2023–2024 peaks (when spreads reached SOFR + 500–600 bps on stressed assets) reflects genuine market improvement: better fundamentals, more liquidity, and more lender competition. But sponsors should set expectations appropriately — all-in rates on bridge loans today will be in the 8.5–10.5% range depending on SOFR levels, spread, and deal profile.

The table below summarizes the current range across deal types:

Deal Type Spread Over SOFR Max LTV Recourse
Multifamily stabilizing, primary market 325–375 bps 75–80% Non-recourse
Multifamily value-add, secondary market 375–450 bps 70–75% Non-recourse
Office transitional (major markets) 450–600 bps 55–65% Limited/full recourse
Industrial / logistics, value-add 300–375 bps 70–75% Non-recourse
Retail repositioning 400–550 bps 60–70% Full recourse typical

Leverage and Proceeds

Maximum LTV on bridge loans has compressed meaningfully from the 2021 peaks. Then, aggressive debt funds were regularly lending to 80–85% of purchase price on well-sponsored deals with strong business plans. Today, 75–80% is the ceiling for best-in-class multifamily, and 65–75% is more typical for other asset classes.

The more important shift is on the future-funding (future advance) component. Bridge loans for value-add assets typically include a future funding facility — the lender advances additional proceeds as renovation work is completed. In 2021, future funding was often structured generously, with lenders underwriting to ambitious pro-forma rent assumptions. Today, future advances are structured more conservatively, with heavier proof-of-performance requirements before funds are released. Sponsors need to budget for slower release of hold-back funds than they may have been accustomed to.

Rate Caps: Still Required, Costs Have Normalized

Every bridge lender of consequence requires a rate cap as a condition of closing. At the peak of the rate cap market (2022–2023), cap premiums on a 2-year, $20M loan at a 4% SOFR strike were running $400K–$600K — a significant drag on closing costs and returns. Those premiums have normalized as volatility has decreased and SOFR has stabilized. Today, comparable caps are running $80K–$150K for well-rated counterparties and reasonable strike rates.

Lenders still require caps at closing and on each extension, and they have become more specific about the counterparty credit rating they'll accept for the cap provider. Build cap costs into your closing and extension cost assumptions.

What Lenders Are Actually Underwriting in 2026

The headline terms — spread, LTV, rate cap — are table stakes. What separates the deals that get done quickly at the best terms from those that drag are the underwriting variables that sophisticated lenders care most about in the current environment:

  • Sponsor track record: This is the single most important factor in 2026. Lenders have been burned by inexperienced sponsors who couldn't execute their business plans during the 2022–2024 stress period. Documented experience with comparable deals — same asset class, same geography, similar business plan — is required, not nice-to-have.
  • Realistic exit analysis: Lenders are stress-testing exit scenarios. If the exit is a refinancing into permanent debt, they're underwriting the permanent loan to conservative assumptions — typically assuming a cap rate 25–75 bps higher than current market. If the exit is a sale, they want evidence of comparable transaction activity.
  • Skin in the game: Equity contributions of at least 20–30% of total project cost are now standard. "Promote" structures where the sponsor contributes minimal equity and participates heavily in the upside are viewed skeptically by lenders who remember the 2022 vintage.
  • Market evidence for rent assumptions: Pro-forma rents must be supported by comparable transactions in the immediate submarket. Lenders are much less willing to underwrite to projected rents without concrete market evidence.
  • Operating reserves: Lenders are requiring more robust operating reserves — both upfront at closing and as ongoing conditions. Sponsors need adequate liquidity throughout the business plan, not just at closing.

Execution Quality: The Variable That Matters Most

In a competitive capital market, many lenders can provide the economics on a given deal. What differentiates firms like Light Tower Group is not the ability to put out feelers to a list of lenders — it's the depth of existing relationships and the ability to manage the process to close.

Bridge loans are complex transactions with long due diligence processes, multiple moving parts (appraisal, environmental, title, insurance, rate cap procurement), and frequent opportunities for a lender to retrade or back away. The value of an advisor isn't in finding the lender; it's in managing the process to make sure the loan closes on the sponsor's timeline at the terms agreed in the term sheet.

We've seen too many sponsors accept a term sheet from the lender with the lowest stated rate, only to find themselves 60 days in with a moving timeline, retraded terms, and no alternative. The best advisors maintain active relationships with 15–20 bridge lenders across the risk spectrum, understand in real time which lenders are capital-constrained or geographically focused, and structure the process so that competitive tension is maintained through closing.

Bottom Line

The bridge market in 2026 is functional, liquid, and actively competitive for quality deals. The window for poorly underwritten deals with inexperienced sponsors at aggressive leverage is closed. But for experienced sponsors with realistic business plans, documented track records, and appropriate equity — the market is there, and the execution is better than it was at any point in 2023 or 2024.

The key is approaching the capital markets strategically: understanding which lenders are most active in your asset class and market, structuring the deal appropriately before going to market, and maintaining competitive tension through closing.
