Bridge vs. Permanent Financing: A Decision Framework

By Benjamin Rohr Â· February 2026 Â· 10 min read

"Should I bridge or go permanent?" is the single most common question sponsors ask when approaching the capital markets. The answer is rarely obvious â€” and getting it wrong can cost millions in unnecessary interest expense or, worse, force a premature sale when a maturity arrives before the business plan is complete.

The Framework: Four Questions

Before diving into rate comparisons and term sheets, sponsors should answer four foundational questions that determine which capital structure is appropriate:

1. Asset Lifecycle Position

This is the threshold question. Permanent financing requires stabilization â€” consistent occupancy (typically 90%+), stable cash flow, and completed capital improvements. If the asset is transitional â€” recently acquired, undergoing renovation, or in lease-up â€” bridge financing is likely the appropriate choice.

The critical nuance: "stabilized" doesn't just mean occupied. It means performing at a level where permanent lenders will underwrite to the in-place cash flow. If there's a meaningful gap between in-place and projected NOI, bridge may be the right choice even for a technically stabilized asset.

2. Business Plan Timeline

Bridge loans typically carry 2â€“3 year initial terms with 1â€“2 year extension options. If your business plan calls for a hold period beyond that â€” say, a 5â€“7 year value-add with a year of renovation, two years of lease-up, and a stabilized hold â€” you need to map the bridge term to the business plan timeline.

The danger zone: sponsors who underestimate their renovation and lease-up timeline and find themselves facing a bridge maturity before stabilization. This is where the real cost of bridge financing materializes â€” not in the rate, but in the extension fees, maturity risk, and potential forced refinancing at an inopportune moment.

3. Full Lifecycle Cost of Capital

This is where sophisticated sponsors separate themselves. The nominal rate on a bridge loan may be 200â€“400 basis points higher than a permanent loan, but the total cost of capital depends on the full picture:

  • Bridge-to-perm: Higher rate for 2â€“3 years, then refinance into a lower permanent rate. Total cost includes origination fees on both loans.
  • Direct to perm: Lower rate from day one, but potentially lower proceeds (70% vs. 80% LTV) and less flexibility for renovations.
  • Blended approach: Permanent first mortgage with mezzanine or preferred equity to achieve higher leverage during the transitional period.

Model all three scenarios across the full hold period, including origination costs, exit fees, and the time value of the additional proceeds that bridge financing provides.

4. Rate Exposure Risk

Bridge loans are floating rate. In a rising rate environment, the interest expense on a floating-rate bridge can increase substantially. Rate caps provide protection, but they have a cost. Permanent loans lock in the rate for the full term.

If rate stability is critical â€” particularly for assets with thin margins or sponsors with limited reserves â€” the certainty of a fixed permanent rate may be worth the lower proceeds.

The Decision Matrix

Based on these four questions, the decision framework becomes clear:

Choose Bridge When:

  • The asset is transitional (value-add, lease-up, renovation)
  • You need higher leverage (75â€“80%+ LTV)
  • The business plan is 2â€“4 years with a clear stabilization exit
  • Speed to close is critical (competitive acquisition)
  • You need flexibility for future funding of renovations

Choose Permanent When:

  • The asset is stabilized with consistent cash flow
  • You want to lock in a rate for 5â€“10+ years
  • The business plan is a long-term hold
  • Maximum proceeds are less important than rate certainty
  • You want to eliminate refinancing risk

Common Mistakes

Over-bridging: Using bridge financing for a stabilized asset because "the rate environment might improve." This is speculation, not business planning. If the asset is stabilized and you plan to hold long-term, lock in permanent financing.

Under-terming: Choosing a 2-year bridge for a 3-year business plan. Always build a cushion. The extension option is not free insurance â€” it's a negotiated right that may come with conditions.

Ignoring total cost: Comparing bridge and permanent rates in isolation. The total cost of capital includes origination, exit fees, rate cap costs, and the refinancing cost when the bridge matures.
