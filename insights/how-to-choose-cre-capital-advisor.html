How to Choose a CRE Capital Advisor: 7 Questions That Separate Good Brokers From Great Ones

The advisor you choose to structure and place your capital doesn't just find you a lender. They define the terms your deal lives and dies on. Before you sign an engagement letter, ask these seven questions.

In commercial real estate, capital is the raw material that makes everything else possible. The right capital structure can unlock a deal. The wrong one — or worse, a deal under-funded or over-leveraged — can kill it.

A CRE capital advisor is supposed to protect you from bad outcomes. But the advisory industry is fragmented and opaque. Some advisors run institutional processes with 40+ lenders in competition. Others cold-call one or two relationships, collect a fee, and call it a day.

The difference between these two experiences is not always obvious from the outside. But the right questions expose it immediately.

The core question: Is this advisor optimizing for my outcome, or for the path of least resistance to their fee? These seven questions will tell you.

Question 1

Who Actually Works My Deal?

This is the first and most important question — and most sponsors don't ask it. At large brokerage shops, the senior partner who pitches you the engagement often hands the deal to a junior associate within days of signing. You negotiated with the principal; the execution is handled by someone with two years of experience.

This matters enormously in capital markets. Lenders receive hundreds of deal briefs a month. A well-structured, clearly articulated loan request from someone they trust — who can answer their questions directly — compresses time to term sheet and improves pricing. A generic package forwarded from an unknown junior analyst goes to the bottom of the pile.

What to listen for: clarity and specificity. If the answer is "our team" or "we have a process," push harder. "My name is on the engagement letter and I personally manage lender outreach through closing" is what you want to hear.

Question 2

How Many Lenders Will You Actually Contact?

A market-clearing process — the kind that produces genuinely competitive terms — requires real competition. That means going wide: presenting your deal to 20, 30, even 50 relevant capital sources, depending on deal size and complexity.

The reason this matters: lenders price deals based on their own internal constraints at a given moment. A life insurance company that hit its multifamily quota last week will pass on a deal they'd have priced very aggressively the month before. A debt fund that just had a payoff will be hungry for volume and price it 25–50 basis points inside their typical range. You don't know which lender is in which position — only a broad outreach reveals it.

Advisors who work a narrow network of four or five "go-to" relationships are not market-clearing your deal. They're convenience-clearing it. You may close — but at suboptimal terms, because the market was never actually tested.

Ask for specifics: "How many lenders in each category — banks, debt funds, life companies, CMBS, agency — will you formally contact for my deal?"

Question 3

Walk Me Through Your Process, Step by Step

A great capital advisor has a repeatable, documented process. They should be able to walk you through it in a conversation without hesitation — because they execute it on every deal, not improvise it each time.

The rough arc of a well-run debt placement process looks like this:

  1. Underwriting: Independently underwrite the deal — not just pass your numbers forward, but stress-test them and identify potential lender concerns before they surface.
  2. Market Positioning: Structure the loan request to lead with strengths and address weaknesses proactively. This is the narrative aspect of capital markets.
  3. Lender Solicitation: Identify and contact 20–50+ relevant capital sources with a well-packaged offering memorandum.
  4. Term Sheet Management: Collect and compare all proposals on an apples-to-apples basis — not just rate, but hold period, prepayment, recourse, reserves, and covenants.
  5. Negotiation: Run a competitive process that drives multiple lenders toward best-and-final pricing.
  6. Due Diligence Coordination: Manage the flow of information between your team and the lender during underwriting.
  7. Closing: Drive to close — coordinating legal, title, inspections, and lender conditions in parallel to compress timeline.

If your advisor's process description skips several of these steps or sounds vague, that's telling you something.

Question 4

Do You Have Preferred Lenders or Referral Relationships?

This is the conflict-of-interest question, and most brokers will not surface it without being asked. Some advisors have formal or informal arrangements with certain lenders — fee-sharing, referral agreements, or simply a relationship so heavily weighted that it effectively creates a preference.

This isn't always disclosed. The result: your deal is presented to the advisor's preferred lender first, given every opportunity to succeed on their terms, and other alternatives are brought in only if necessary to create the appearance of competition.

You want an advisor who has relationships with many lenders, but preference for none of them. The right lender for your deal should emerge from a competitive process — not be predetermined by the advisor's financial relationship with one institution.

Ask directly: "Do you have any financial arrangements with lenders — referral fees, revenue sharing, or preferred placement agreements?" A trustworthy advisor answers clearly. Evasiveness is a data point.

Question 5

Can You Show Me Comparable Closings?

Track record is the most powerful signal of advisor competency. Not just deal volume — the specific types of deals they've successfully placed, in the markets relevant to your asset, with the capital structures that match your need.

A great advisor can immediately point to specific closings: "We placed a $35M bridge loan on a value-add multifamily in that submarket eighteen months ago at 200 over SOFR, full-term I/O, no recourse. Here's the structure." That specificity comes from having actually done the work.

Watch for vague generalities — "we've done a lot of multifamily" or "we have strong lender relationships in that market." These are easy to say. Ask for specifics: deal size, asset class, market, capital structure, lender type, timeline to close. If they can't answer specifically, their track record in your deal type may be thinner than their pitch suggests.

Related: check their track record independently where possible. LinkedIn, published tombstones, or references from previous clients are all legitimate due diligence steps before signing an engagement letter.

Question 6

How Do You Get Paid, and When?

Fee structure in capital advisory matters for two reasons: alignment and transparency.

Alignment: A success-fee-only structure — where the advisor earns their fee only when you close — is the purest form of alignment. If they don't close, they don't get paid. Their incentive is identical to yours. Retainer-based models or upfront fees introduce a more complex dynamic — the advisor has already been compensated before the deal is done.

Transparency: Fee structures in this business are not always disclosed upfront in full. Ask specifically: What is the advisory fee as a percentage of loan proceeds? Is there a minimum fee? Are there any fees paid by the lender to you or your firm? Are there any marketing or administrative fees outside of the success fee?

Standard advisory fees for debt placement range from approximately 0.5% to 1.5% of loan proceeds, depending on deal complexity, loan size, and market. Equity placement fees are often higher, typically 1–3% of equity raised. Anything significantly outside these ranges warrants a conversation about what's included.

Question 7

What Happens If the Deal Falls Apart?

Deals fall apart. Appraisals come in low. Lenders re-trade at the last minute. Borrowers' credit profiles shift during due diligence. Markets move. A great advisor has navigated these situations before — and how they handle them reveals more about their value than any smooth process ever could.

Ask: "Walk me through a deal that went sideways and how you handled it." Listen for candor, creativity, and persistence. Did they re-market the deal to a different lender pool? Did they restructure the ask to fit new market conditions? Did they counsel the sponsor to walk away from a deal that no longer penciled?

An advisor who has only worked deals that went smoothly hasn't been tested by the market yet. The advisory value is most visible when things don't go to plan — and you want an advisor who has the experience, relationships, and judgment to navigate that terrain.

Also ask about the engagement letter terms if a deal falls apart: Is the fee earned upon signing? Upon term sheet? Upon closing? What are the termination provisions? Understanding this upfront eliminates unpleasant surprises later.

What Great Looks Like

A great capital advisor is, fundamentally, an extension of your own judgment in a market you may not have full visibility into. They should know more about the current lending environment than you do — what's pricing, what's tightening, which lenders are hungry, and which are pulling back. And they should translate that knowledge into specific advantages for your deal.

They should surface problems before lenders do, protect your process from re-trades, and still be on the phone with you the week before closing making sure nothing falls through the cracks.

The questions above will not guarantee you find that advisor on the first try. But they will eliminate the fee-collectors quickly — and that alone is worth the effort before you sign.
