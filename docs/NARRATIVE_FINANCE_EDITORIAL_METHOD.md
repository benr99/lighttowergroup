# Narrative-finance editorial method

Light Tower writes about capital markets as decisions made under pressure.
The goal is neither dry technical recap nor theatrical business gossip. It is
to make the machinery of a deal legible by following the people, constraints,
and clocks that reveal it.

## The story engine

Every Insight begins with an internal six-part brief:

1. **Anchor** — the reported deal, number, filing, building, or policy action.
2. **Tension** — the economically consequential contradiction or pressure.
3. **Cast** — parties with different needs, clocks, and risk positions.
4. **Mechanism** — the basis, debt, liquidity, regulation, or operating fact
   that produces the pressure.
5. **Claim** — the bounded interpretation Light Tower can defend.
6. **Reader consequence** — what an owner, lender, sponsor, operator, or
   investor should test in their own work.

The mode rotates—basis, lender behavior, city form, timing, counterparties, or
an assumption under pressure—so a reader receives an edited intelligence
product rather than a repeated template.

## Reporting discipline

Each draft keeps three categories distinct:

- **Reported facts:** names, figures, chronology, filings, quotations, and
  source-supported physical details.
- **Interpretations:** reasoned explanations of incentives or financial
  consequences, phrased as analysis rather than hidden fact.
- **Open questions:** material items the source cannot establish.

A scene can carry a reader into the piece only when its concrete detail is in
the reporting. We never manufacture a site visit, a conversation, a sensory
detail, or an insider anecdote to create drama.

## Writing moves

- Open on pressure, not the press release.
- Explain what a structure permits or prevents: a maturity creates a clock; a
  ground lease changes control; preferred equity changes claims on upside; a
  policy decision changes the underwriting math.
- Use the character who best exposes the mechanism, but describe the person’s
  role and decision—not an invented personality.
- Mix short verdicts with fact-rich sentences. Put the important number or
  action early. Let a strong sentence stand alone when it earns the space.
- Use a precise image, metaphor, or dry aside only when it clarifies a fact.
  One good line is more valuable than a string of jokes.

## The Light Tower posture

Warm but not soft. Skeptical but not cynical. Confident but never omniscient.
Distress is a capital-structure problem involving people, not a spectacle.
We do not posture as gossip insiders, market a transaction, or turn one deal
into proof of a market-wide thesis.

## Quality gate

Before publication, the automated ledger must identify the six story elements,
separate fact from inference, and give provenance for any scene. The independent
review also rejects template language, corrupted typography, incomplete drafts,
and unsupported fallback output. Failed AI drafts receive one targeted revision
against the actual finding; otherwise they are held for an editor.
